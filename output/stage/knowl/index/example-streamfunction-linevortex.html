Example 4.2.9. Line vortex.

The fundamental solution for the streamfunction, in plane polar coordinates, is the axisymmetric solution,
\begin{equation} \psi = -\frac{\Gamma}{2\pi}\log r,\tag{4.2.8} \end{equation}
defined up to a constant, and corresponds to a line vortex.

Recall that the radial and angular velocities are given by (4.2.6). Therefore, we see that the velocity vector is given by
\begin{equation*} \bu = -\pd{\psi}{r} \be_{\theta} = \frac{\Gamma}{2\pi r} \be_{\theta}, \end{equation*}
and thus this flow corresponds to entirely circular trajectories orbiting the origin, and with angular velocity increasing as \(r \to 0\text{.}\)

The quantity \(\Gamma\) is called the vortex strength, analogous to the source strength \(Q\) in ExampleÂ 4.1.7. Let us consider the amount of circulation around a contour \(C\) that contains the origin:
\begin{equation*} \oint_C \bu \cdot \de{\bx} = \oint_C (u \, \de{x} + v \, \de{y}), \end{equation*}
i.e. one envisions encircling the origin along \(C\text{,}\) adding up each of the velocity components tangential to the path. This is the circulation. By Stokesâ€™ theorem it is equal to the flux of vorticity of the corresponding bounding surface.
ðŸ”—
Choosing \(C\) to be the circle of radius \(r = a\text{,}\) we have \(\de{\bx} = a \be_{\theta} \de{\theta}\text{.}\) Then the circulation is given by
\begin{equation*} \oint_C \bu \cdot \de{\bx} = \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{\Gamma}{2\pi a} \be_{\theta} \cdot \be_{\theta} a \de\theta = \Gamma. \end{equation*}
So indeed this gives us an intuitive understanding of \(\Gamma\text{.}\) Notice that \(\Gamma \gt 0\) corresponds to flow in the anticlockwise sense, and \(\Gamma \lt 0\) corresponds to flow in the clockwise sense.
